1.1 On finding a topic

We find it difficult to pin our interests onto one specific topic. We would like to set up an experiment capable of observing the atomic structure, i.e. to see the fine structure– or hyperfine structure splitting. To set up an experiment that is capable of measuring these incredibly detailed measurements of physical structures we need to focus on the tools that we use to analyse the atomic structure of Rubidium.

In our opinion, to make the extremely detailed measurement of a atomic structure, a detailed understanding of the external-cavity diode-laser (ECDL) and its emission spectrum is required. Detailed knowledge on the diode laser should provide us with the means to setup a ideal frequency to observe the fine–, hyperfine structure. But how do we characterize the light of the ECDL?

This brings us to the issue on spectroscopy. The Fabry-Pérot interferometer only measures relative spectra – though very precisely. To correctly characterize the emitted light of the ECDL, it seems advantageous to gain a deep understanding and a characterization of the Fabry-Pérot interferometer, which allows for the full characterization of the diode laser. The characterization of the ECDL go hand-in-hand with the characterization of the Fabry-Pérot interferometer.

The last point to be considered is optics. Not all optical components need to be understood in great detail to characterize the ECDL, though some shall be essential, i.e. a lense as well as the diffraction grating on a mirror are an integral part of the ECDL.

The set goal should be to fully characterize the ECDL, which by necessity shall include the characterization of other parts to the setup of the experimental set up to EIT. This means, we want to:

  • compare emission spectrum of ECDL with theoretical prediction of semiconductor physics:
    • How does the population inversion take place?
    • What is our semi conductor made of (i.e. GaAs)?
    • Is a characterization of the semi-conductor possible (\(I–U\))?
    • What instruments do we have to measure the spectrum?
    • What is the Free spectral range of the ECDL (Fabry-Pérot modes)?
    • Temperature dependance of emitted spectrum?
    • Can we determine the diffraction index \(n\) of the depletion region (optical fiber)

As concrete next steps, we need to find out about material compositions and the dimensions of the diode to then understand the models that describe the emission spectrum to make numerical predictions.

4.6.2 Single Mode and Coherence Control factors that affect the ECDL mode frequency/wavelength

  1. medium gain profile (of the diode gain medium)
  2. internal cavity i.e. diode length \(l\)
  3. external resonator mode defined by \(L\) (optical distance of rear internal cavity mirror to the grating)
  4. grating profile of the grated mirror

Threshold Current
\(I_{th}\) threshold point of input current to diode at which it begins to lase. low threshold current means more efficient diode.
P-I Curve
\(frac{\Delta P}{\Delta T}\) It shows change in output power over change in input current. It is also refered as L-I curve, Where L refers to luminosity, and I to current. P is measure of luminosity in watt, I is measure of current

Luminosity :Typically it refers to typical optical output power produced by given amount of input current.

External Differential Quantum Efficiency
\(\eta_d\) indicates the ability of laser device to convert input current(injected electron hole pair) to photon emitted from the device(output light) \(\eta_d=frac{\Delta P/Delta I}{hc/q \lambda}\) h is planks constant, is wavelength, c is speed of light, q is charge of electron. When talking about External Differential Quantum Efficiency one should always note if diode is one mirror facet or two mirror facet.
Perfect Hypothetical Device
The device that works with 100% efficiency without wasting energy in any unrequired form.
Current Density
\(J\) is quantity which is ratio of current to area(area is consist of length of diode and width of ridge(we need to have better defination of it)). Density is more useful quantity when comparing different diodes.
Internal Quantum Efficiency
\(\eta_i\) It is measure of efficiency of laser diode in convertng electron hole pairs(input current) into photons(light) within laser diode structure. \(\eta_i\) is independet of geometrical properties of laser device, which makes it proper parameter for comparision of material quality of various laser devices. It is determined by plotting inverse external differential quantum efficiency(y axis) vs cavity length(x axis)

Once we have \(\eta_d\) and \(\eta_i\) we can calculate ratio \(\frac{eta_d}{\eta_i}\) which is photon generated to photon emitted ratio.

Internal Loss of Laser Diode
Parameter that corresponds to loss of optical wave(need to know more about it)

Characteristic Temperature \(T_o\) It is measure of temperature sensitivity of Laser Diode. Higher values of T_o imply that thr threshold current density and external differential quantum efficiency of laser diode increases rapidly with increasing temperatures(meaning laser diode being more thermally stable.) to measure characteristic temperature we need to measure P-I curve at different temperatures. —

We approach this reflection from the position that it is good to be in a lost state of mind — that this is the only state that truly exists, though we usually trick ourselves into thinking otherwise. 7.2.2 Finding a topic

What do we want to understand? – is the first step for any scientific work. We went about this, by looking at the components of our experiment:

  • laser physics: external-cavity diode-laser (ECDL)
  • atomic physics: \(^{87}\text{Rn}(I=3/2)\), \(^{85}\text{Rn}(I=5/2)\)
  • optics: alignment and optical components
    • linear polarizer
    • quarter wave plate
    • half wave plate
    • lense
    • Fabry-Pérot interferometer
  • spectroscopy techniques: Absorption, Flourescence, and Saturation
  • interferometery: Fabry-Pérot interferometer

From these topics we identified our interests. During the discussion we realized that the underlying tool for our experiment is the external-cavity diode-laser (ECDL). A consensus was reached to study the external-cavity diode-laser in detail. It was the goal to get an in depth understanding of our device, and be able to manipulate the laser through controllable parameters. This, eventually led us to attempt a numerical simulation and modeling the ECDL. This decision process was finalized with a written document, that summarized our thoughts, discussions as well as holding onto concrete next steps.

In this discussion we came across a issues that highlight the recursive manner of scientific work. This is, that another fundamental tool of the experiment is required. The tool for obervations. These were the photo-diode and an interferometer, or Fabry-Pérot interferometer, which would be used to characterize the ECDL. Therefore, we faced the dilemma that required us to have a thorough understanding of these instruments, simultaneous to the understanding of the ECDL. This dilemma, though was not further discussed until the end of the two week lab time.

“We find it difficult to pin our interests onto one specific topic. We would like to set up an experiment capable of observing the atomic structure, i.e. to see the fine structure– or hyperfine structure splitting. To set up an experiment that is capable of measuring these incredibly detailed measurements of physical structures we need to focus on the tools that we use to analyse the atomic structure of Rubidium.

In our opinion, to make the extremely detailed measurement of a atomic structure, a detailed understanding of the external-cavity diode-laser (ECDL) and its emission spectrum is required. Detailed knowledge on the diode laser should provide us with the means to setup a ideal frequency to observe the fine–, hyperfine structure. But how do we characterize the light of the ECDL?

This brings us to the issue on spectroscopy. The Fabry-Pérot interferometer only measures relative spectra – though very precisely. To correctly characterize the emitted light of the ECDL, it seems advantageous to gain a deep understanding and a characterization of the Fabry-Pérot interferometer, which allows for the full characterization of the diode laser. The characterization of the ECDL go hand-in-hand with the characterization of the Fabry-Pérot interferometer.

The last point to be considered is optics. Not all optical components need to be understood in great detail to characterize the ECDL, though some shall be essential, i.e. a lense as well as the diffraction grating on a mirror are an integral part of the ECDL.

The set goal should be to fully characterize the ECDL, which by necessity shall include the characterization of other parts to the setup of the experimental set up to EIT. This means, we want to:

  • compare emission spectrum of ECDL with theoretical prediction of semiconductor physics:
    • How does the population inversion take place?
    • What is our semi conductor made of (i.e. GaAs)?
    • Is a characterization of the semi-conductor possible (\(I–U\))?
    • What instruments do we have to measure the spectrum?
    • What is the Free spectral range of the ECDL (Fabry-Pérot modes)?
    • Temperature dependance of emitted spectrum?
    • Can we determine the diffraction index \(n\) of the depletion region (optical fiber)

As concrete next steps, we need to find out about material compositions and the dimensions of the diode to then understand the models that describe the emission spectrum to make numerical predictions.”

When delving into the literature, we realized that some of the task we set ourselves, could not be achieved. It appeared, that the parameters and dimension of the diode, that were expected to be easily obtained were not given in the handbook.

Retrospectively it would require to document this observation and to redefine our task. Though we felt the urge to continue the literature research. This issue will become apparent in the following section.

7.3 Reflection and Issues

Our two-week journey was never short of difficulties. This made us realize how difficult the scientific process itself could be. Among all other major and minor issues, a prominent one was the drive and motivation to stay optimistic every day.

Our backgrounds as individuals clearly affect our understanding. A different upbringing should not be taken lightly: “D” grew up in Germany with a bilingual English/German upbringing, while “A” grew up in Pakistan in a multilingual surrounding. In a physics project our interests as physicists gave rise to the starting points. These needed to be agreed upon and merged in order to work on the same problem. Luckily, the two of us have a similar interest in physics. We studied similar courses with interests in abstract mathematical concepts. Hence our choice for a deeper theoretical understanding of the ECDL.

One of the core issues we can pinpoint was finding structure in our progress during times of disagreement. When we did not set anything as definite truth, all statements seemed to become equal truths. This made it difficult to decide how to move forward.

This difficulty was connected to the way the two-week activity was redefined for us. Usually, such an activity might be understood as working toward a clear goal, result, or presentation. However, UW told us there were no expectations, which made it both tricky and difficult to understand what our direction should be. The impression we had was that the activity was about learning the learning-process, which made us look inward. An inward looking, introspective task. One is usually introspective toward past actions, not present ones. Trying to be introspective while still being inside the process made us more aware of ourselves. Though also unsure about what to do next, and how to assess ourselves while doing it.

Being used to expectations and the developed habit to fullfill the expectations of our superior under timeconstraints could bot be overlooked. Whilst trying adopt to the new structure, the time constraint remained. Which toward the deadline resulted in the difficulty of maintaining motivation and optimism. This became especially clear, when progress, in the sense of advancing, seeing results, or having something to present to a third person, was not reached. Furthermore in a time-constrained working environment, one cannot explore and deviate much. The feeling of having little time left for any further work led us to stutter and come to a halt. At that point, the lack of confidence in our knowledge was prominent, and simple philosophical questions made us question the most basic thing: What are we doing here? And why are we doing what we were doing?

Throughout the entire process, a recurring conflict that we observed and tried to understand was our expectations of ourselves as individuals. However, what exactly these differences were, and where they came from, was not easy to identify. So far, we can formulate them through two statements. On the one hand: “I feel like I am free, so I want to make the most out of this opportunity.” On the other hand: “I felt like, as there are no expectations, I would just want to enjoy the process of learning without really worrying about doing a lot.” This made us question, how we could find a middle ground in terms of our expectations?

Another important issue was about communication: How to express thoughts in the best possible way? How to make sure that information was transferred clearly and without too much deviation? As well as, how to know whether we had reached the same understanding? This also raised the question of how we come to agreement when our interpretations of certain concepts are not exactly the same.

This became especially visible when trying to define concepts like “semiconductor.” Our language background, mentioned above, defined our boundary conditions. To agree upon the same problem, the problem needs to be defined without missunderstanding.

A definition is given in relation to common words and technical words, which, when defined, are related to other common words and technical words, and so on and so forth. In the end, one creates a web of relations that defines technical terms through this relation in terms of common words. Common words are those that seem natural and need no further explanation, for example:

semiconductor
A material that can be tuned, i.e. voltage V, to switch between two macroscopic behaviors: insulator and conductor.

Common words here could arguably be “material,” “switch,” or “behaviors.” Of course, this can maybe be avoided by defining these terms as well, though there are two issues. One is the practicality under time constraints whilst wanting to learn physics. Secondly, there is the question about the actual possibility of clearly defining without the use of technical terms that need no definition, which we, the authors, do not believe is possible. Further investigation of this would be interesting.

Our differences were not only scientific but also aesthetic in nature. At times, our disagreements were not about right or wrong, but about different perspectives. In terms of aesthetics, we felt that we both had different preferences, which expressed themselves in our expectations of progress and were also visible in the glossary section while coming to consensus about definitions. In that sense, we did not feel that either of us was right or wrong, but these differences still influenced the goal we wanted to achieve and how we could form consensus over it? More importantly, this made us question, how these expectations reflected in our struggle to work scientifically together? And what we could do to overcome this?

Progress got sidetracked frequently. By the idea of creating a numerical simulation for future students. As well as a deep dive into one specific literature, rather than collecting various literature. This bahaviour can be explained by us ignoring the question if this aligns with the way we wanted to proceed originally. Which resulted in an observable change within our notes, as details on “how to use the DLpro” or “tuning of the diode laser” were gathered in the hope of being helpful to others in the future. Also, “important” notes such as graphs by the manufacturer, capturing the input and output parameters, were collected.

7.4 Conclusion

It is good to be in this lost state of mind because this is the only state that truly exists, though we usually trick ourselves into thinking otherwise – is what we said on the last day in the lab course. This captures our aesthetic toward scientific work itself: That we are all lost wonderers finding our way. And yet the university is not structured to embrace this. It demands performed confidence, compliance with expectation, and deference to hierarchy, narrowing the variety of ideas and reinforcing itself in a feedback loop that is difficult to escape. Even in a lab unit explicitly designed to rupture these structures — offering us a free choice, an ‘ignorant’ supervisor, and the space to follow our own interests — we found that our habits that developed in the conventional academic culture do not loosen easily. They simply became more visible. This lab unit was, in both senses of the word, political: it demanded that we navigate our own decision-making process, and it ruptured the common understanding of what a lab course is. Finally, this report can become political in a third sense. We offer it not as a document to be graded and judged from above, but as feedback on equal terms — a serious attempt to reflect on how the lab course might better achieve what it sets out to do. Breaking the habits of conventional academic culture requires not only that students be willing to let go of old ways of working, but that the course itself remains open to critique and change from those who pass through it.

This experimental class was about learning how to work scientifically as a team, and it stood in sharp contrast to the usual school-like environment. That of fulfilling expectations of seniors and supervisors in order to pass or to get good grades. Eventhough we appreciated the change, we struggled to adapt. The decision processes between the two of us — how to reach common understanding, how to move forward together — was itself a form of politics, and one we had little prior experience navigating.

In the first week, our lack of experience with Github cost a few days, and our struggle can be seen particularly in the history of the branches. We described how we came to a conclusion for the topic, this progress was summarized in written form. With observing that the continouse progress deviated to the set goal by ourselves when continuing in the literature review. Not only did we deviate from our own goals we defined, but also did our common knowlagde. We worked independantly not taking the time to see: Are we in line with our previous tasks? No! So what changed? Then rephrase our question, in order to reorientate ourselves – in order not to “vor lauter Bäume den Wald nicht sehen”.

For the second week we did define how to proceed, though we did not bridge the knowladge-gap that had developed in the first week. From here on our work became chaotic, our ideas and motives changed, and a measurement we started resulted in more uncertainty. Our goals were clearly not appropriate to the time constraint, our notes did not catch up with our discussions and we came to realize that the differences posed an issue for further advancements.

From this we can take away several lessons. It could be advantageous to put more effort into taking notes and structuring our thoughts: using two sets of notes, one with unordered thoughts and explanation, and one that is more organized and structured, capturing the main line of argumentation and describing where we find ourselves and where we wish to go. There should be space for creative working, where there is space to drift but also the means to track it and to re-adjust the goals appropriately. The scientific progress is slow and draining, and the expectation of quick results do not help. While working independently it is important to exchange in written form where we can explain to each other what we learned. This would have helped in two manners: building the bridge of understanding, and helping us to be more confident in our understanding of what we read. These sessions are also where the deliberate practice of consensus could have been sustained more consistently. We kept a glossary, though independently, they were not merged to one. The issues of ego, if addressed while working, can help to make the working environment healthy — and addressing ego is itself a rupture of the assumption that scientific collaboration is purely intellectual, and that the personal does not enter into it. It does.

8 Glossary

8.0.1 Concepts

diode
A diode is a semiconductor. Made by glueing at least two types of p– and n-doped materials. further details required
semiconductor
A material that can be tuned (i.e. Voltage \(V\)) to switch between two macroscopic behaviors: isolator & conductor
laser
Light amplification by stimulated emission of radiation. Emits light with high spacial and temporal coherence.
ECDL
External Cavity Diode Laser. This is a laser with a diode at its core and an external cavity resulting in the emission of light with thin spectral width.
FPI
Fabry-Pérot interferometer. description
beam cross-section
The beam cross-section is the geometric form that describes the intensity profile of laser in the plane spanned by the two vectors that are mutually orthogonal to the wave-vector
medium \(n\)
Referes to the environment of the physical system. The environment can get characterized i.e. by the refractive index \(n\).
gain profile
The emission spectrum of the diode, i.e. the emission spectrum without cavities (internal or external).
cavity
Is a space defined by boundary conditions which defines allowed excitation energies of the radiation field. The cavity defines the boundary conditions of the harmonic oscillator.
internal cavity \(l\)
The cavity made from two mirrors opposite to each other with a defining parameter of length \(l\) that is defined by the geometry of the diode. The internal cavity arises partially due to an applied coating with high reflectivity though also due to the reflection due to the difference of refractive index (medium to air).
internal mode
The spectrum resulting from the internal cavity.
external cavity \(L\)
The cavity which is established by an external mirror and runs from the further end of the internal cavity to the external mirror.
external mode
The spectrum resulting from the external cavity.
grating profile
The profile of the grated mirror, it depends on the incoming angle \(\alpha_g\) of the light to the grating.
equilibrium
For a system to find its rest position in state space.
